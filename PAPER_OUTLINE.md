# Storage-Water Quality Paper Outline

## Working Title

Storage architecture organizes stream chemistry fingerprints and synchrony in
H.J. Andrews headwater catchments

## Main Idea

Headwater catchments differ not only in how much water they store, but in the
way storage is structured, drained, mixed, and connected to streams. Those
storage differences should leave a chemical imprint.

In this paper, we test whether the storage metrics developed in the HJA storage
paper help explain two dimensions of stream chemistry:

- site-level chemistry fingerprints
- pairwise chemical synchrony

## Abstract Draft

Stream chemistry integrates hydrologic flow paths, water residence times, and
biogeochemical source areas, yet it remains difficult to predict why nearby
headwater catchments differ in chemical behavior or why some catchments respond
together through time. Building on a multi-metric storage framework developed
for H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest catchments, we tested whether finalized
storage metrics explain variation in stream chemistry fingerprints and chemical
synchrony across long-term monitoring sites. We combined long-term stream
chemistry and discharge records with storage metrics describing flashiness,
recession behavior, flow-duration behavior, dynamic storage, water balance,
baseflow fraction, isotope damping, young-water fraction, and mean transit time.
We used PCA to characterize site-level chemistry behavior and pairwise synchrony
analyses to test whether catchments with similar storage metrics had more
synchronized chemistry. This framework links catchment storage architecture to
stream chemical organization and provides a bridge between hydrologic storage
dynamics and biogeochemical response.

## Research Questions

- Do HJA catchments have different chemistry fingerprints?
- Do storage metrics align with chemistry behavior?
- Are catchments with more similar storage metrics more chemically synchronized?

## Hypotheses

We expect catchments to separate in chemistry fingerprint space because
differences in storage, flow paths, and residence times should produce distinct
C-Q and concentration patterns.

We expect storage metrics related to drainage behavior, mixing, and residence
time to align with major chemistry axes. Metrics such as RCS, FDC, SD, WB, BF,
DR, Fyw, and MTT should help identify whether chemical differences reflect
flashiness, drainage structure, storage buffering, or water age.

Finally, we expect site pairs with more similar storage metrics to have stronger
chemical synchrony. If two catchments drain, mix, and store water similarly,
their chemistry should be more likely to rise and fall together through time.

## Results Outline

### Result 1. HJA Streams Occupy Distinct Chemistry Fingerprint Space

Use the all-solute PCA to show that sites are not chemically interchangeable.
The goal is not to overinterpret every solute vector, but to show that each site
has a multivariate chemical fingerprint.

### Result 2. Storage Metrics Align With Chemistry Fingerprints

Overlay RBI, RCS, FDC, SD, WB, BF, DR, Fyw, and MTT as vectors on the chemistry
ordination.

This connects the water-quality analysis directly to the storage paper.

### Result 3. Storage Similarity Predicts Chemical Synchrony

Use annual pairwise concentration synchrony as the measured synchrony response.

Negative correlations between storage-metric distance and synchrony mean that
site pairs that are more different in storage behavior are less chemically
synchronized.
